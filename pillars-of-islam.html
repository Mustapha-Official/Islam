The Principles of Islam

The Five Pillars Of Islam With Deep Explanations

Narrated Ibn 'Umar: Allah's Messenger (ﷺ) said: Islam is based on (the following) five (principles): 1. To testify that none has the right to be worshipped but Allah and Muhammad is Allah's Messenger (ﷺ). 2. To offer the (compulsory congregational) prayers dutifully and perfectly. 3. To pay Zakat (i.e. obligatory charity) . 4. To perform Hajj. (i.e. Pilgrimage to Mecca) 5. To observe fast during the month of Ramadan.


Reference : Sahih al-Bukhari 8

1. Shahadah (الشهادة) – The Declaration of Faith


What is the shahadah?


The Shahada is the first pillar of Islam. It is the declaration that a person believes in the oneness of Allah and accepts Muhammad (ﷺ) as Allah's final Messenger. The Shahada in Arabic:


لَا إِلَٰهَ إِلَّا ٱللَّٰهُ، مُحَمَّدٌ رَسُولُ ٱللَّٰهِ


Transliteration> Lā ilāha illā Allāh, Muḥammadur Rasūlullāh.
English Meaning> "There is no deity worthy of worship except Allah, and Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah."

This statement is the foundation of Islam. A person enters Islam by sincerely believing it in their heart and declaring it with understanding.


Breaking the Shahadah into Two Parts


Part 1: "There is no deity worthy of worship except Allah."

This teaches Tawhid—the absolute oneness of Allah. It means:

  • Allah alone created everything.
  • Allah alone deserves worship.
  • No person, idol, saint, angel, prophet, or object should be worshipped alongside Allah.
  • Muslims direct acts of worship such as prayer, supplication, hope, trust, and sacrifice to Allah alone.
Allah says in the Qur'an:>
"And your God is One God. There is no deity except Him, the Most Merciful, the Especially Merciful."
(Surah Al-Baqarah 2:163)

Part 2: "Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah."

This means Muslims believe that Muhammad (ﷺ) was chosen by Allah to deliver His final revelation, the Qur'an. Believing this means:

  • Accepting his teachings.
  • Following his authentic example (the Sunnah).
  • Loving and respecting him without worshipping him.
  • Believing he is the final prophet sent by Allah.
Allah says:
"Muhammad is not the father of any of your men, but he is the Messenger of Allah and the Seal of the Prophets."
(Surah Al-Ahzab 33:40)


Why is the Shahadah important?


The Shahada is the foundation upon which the other pillars are built. Without sincere faith:
Prayer has no foundation, Charity has no foundation, Fasting has no foundation, Pilgrimage has no foundation, Faith comes first, then worship follows.


What does the Shahadah change in a Muslim's life?


A Muslim who lives by the Shahada strives to:

  • Worship Allah alone.
  • Speak truthfully.
  • Be honest.
  • Show kindness.
  • Keep promises.
  • Avoid injustice.
  • Follow the teachings of Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ).
The Shahada is not just words—it is a commitment that shapes a person's beliefs, worship, and character.

Key Lessons

  • Allah is One and has no partners.
  • Only Allah deserves worship.
  • Muhammad (ﷺ) is Allah's final Messenger.
  • The Qur'an is Allah's final revealed Book.
  • The Shahadah is the first and most fundamental pillar of Islam.

2. Salah (الصلاة) - The Five Daily Prayers


What is Salah?


Salah is the second pillar of Islam. It is the obligatory act of worship in which Muslims pray to Allah five times every day at prescribed times.

Salah is a direct connection between a servant and their Creator. It reminds Muslims of Allah throughout the day and strengthens their faith, discipline, gratitude, and humility.

Allah says in the Qur'an:
"Indeed, prayer has been decreed upon the believers at specified times."
(Surah An-Nisa 4:103)

The Five Daily Prayers


1. Fajr
Before sunrise.
2 obligatory (fard) units (rak'ahs).
Begins the day with the remembrance of Allah.

2. Dhuhr
After the sun passes its highest point (midday).
4 obligatory rak'ahs.
A reminder to pause from daily work and worship Allah.

3. Asr
Late afternoon.
4 obligatory rak'ahs.
Encourages patience and continued remembrance of Allah.

4. Magrib
Immediately after sunset.
3 obligatory rak'ahs.
A time to thank Allah as the day ends.

5. Isha
Night.
4 obligatory rak'ahs.
Ends the day with worship and seeking Allah's forgiveness.


Why do Muslims pray?


Muslims pray because Allah has commanded it. Salah also:

  • Brings the heart closer to Allah.
  • Strengthens faith and trust in Him.
  • Teaches discipline and punctuality.
  • Helps a person stay away from sinful actions.
  • Brings peace and comfort to the soul.
  • Unites Muslims, especially in congregation.
Allah says:
> "Establish prayer for My remembrance."
(Surah Taha 20:14)
Allah also says:
"Indeed, prayer restrains from shameful and unjust deeds."

(Surah Al-'Ankabut 29:45)


Conditions before Salah


Before praying, a Muslim should:

  • Have sincere intention (niyyah).
  • Be in a state of purification by performing wudu, or ghusl when required.
  • Ensure the body, clothes, and prayer place are clean.
  • Cover the awrah (parts of the body that should be covered in prayer).
  • Face the Qiblah (the direction of the Kaaba in Makkah).
  • Pray within the correct prayer time.


The importance of Salah


Salah was made obligatory during the Prophet Muhammad's (ﷺ) Night Journey and Ascension (Al-Isra' wal-Mi'raj). It is unique among the pillars because it was commanded directly by Allah during this miraculous event.

The Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) said:
> "The first deed for which a person will be brought to account on the Day of Resurrection is the prayer."
(Reported in collections including Abu Dawud and At-Tirmidhi.)

Lessons from Salah


Allah should be remembered throughout every day.
Worship requires sincerity, humility, and consistency.
Time is a trust and should be used wisely.
Prayer strengthens both the individual and the Muslim community.

3. Zakat (الزكاة) – Obligatory Charity


What is Zakat?


Zakat is the third pillar of Islam. It is an obligatory act of worship in which eligible Muslims give a portion of their wealth each year to those who have the right to receive it.

The word "Zakat" means purification and growth. By giving Zakat sincerely for the sake of Allah, a Muslim purifies their wealth and their heart from greed while helping those in need.

Allah says in the Qur'an:
>"Establish prayer and give Zakat..."
(Surah Al-Baqarah 2:43)


Why is Zakat important?


Zakat teaches that all wealth ultimately belongs to Allah, and people are entrusted with it. It helps to:

  • Purify a Muslim's wealth.
  • Support the poor and needy.
  • Reduce poverty and hardship.
  • Strengthen love and unity within the Muslim community.
  • Encourage generosity and gratitude.


Who must pay Zakat?


A Muslim must pay Zakat if they:

  • Are Muslim.
  • Own wealth above the minimum amount (Nisab).
  • Have possessed that wealth for one lunar year (Hawl).
  • If a person's wealth is below the Nisab, they are not required to pay Zakat.


How much is Zakat?


The standard rate of Zakat on qualifying savings and many forms of wealth is:
> 2.5% (one-fortieth)
For example:
Savings of 10,000 → Zakat = 250
Savings of 100,000 → Zakat = 2,500

(The exact calculation can vary depending on the type of assets, so many Muslims use a Zakat calculator or seek guidance for more complex situations.)


Who can receive Zakat?


The Qur'an mentions categories of people who may receive Zakat, including:

  • The poor.
  • The needy.
  • Those employed to administer Zakat.
  • Those whose hearts are to be reconciled.
  • Those in bondage (where applicable).
  • In the cause of Allah (as understood within Islamic teachings).
  • The stranded traveler.
Allah says:
> "Zakat expenditures are only for the poor, the needy..."
(Surah At-Tawbah 9:60)


Zakat and Sadaqah


Many people confuse these two.

Zakat
Obligatory.
One of the Five Pillars of Islam.
Given by eligible Muslims according to Islamic rules.

Sadaqah
Voluntary charity.
Can be given at any time.
Has no fixed amount.

Both are rewarded by Allah, but only Zakat fulfills the obligation of the third pillar.


Benefits of Zakat


  • Purifies wealth and the heart.
  • Brings blessings (barakah) to wealth.
  • Helps those in genuine need.
  • Reduces inequality.
  • Reminds Muslims to be thankful for Allah's blessings.


Key Lessons


Wealth is a trust from Allah.
Giving to others is an act of worship.
Caring for the poor is a responsibility.
Generosity strengthens society.
Zakat is an obligation for eligible Muslims, not merely a recommendation.

4. Sawm (الصوم) – Fasting in Ramadan


What is Sawm?


Sawm is the fourth pillar of Islam. It is the act of fasting during the month of Ramadan, the ninth month of the Islamic lunar calendar.

From dawn (Fajr) until sunset (Maghrib), Muslims abstain from:
Food, Drink, Smoking, Marital relations

Fasting is not only about avoiding food and drink—it is also about increasing worship, self-discipline, patience, and closeness to Allah.

Allah says in the Qur'an:
> "O you who believe! Fasting has been prescribed for you as it was prescribed for those before you, so that you may attain Taqwa (God-consciousness)."
(Surah Al-Baqarah 2:183)


Why do Muslims fast?


Muslims fast because Allah has commanded it. Fasting helps a Muslim to:

  • Develop Taqwa (awareness and consciousness of Allah).
  • Strengthen patience and self-control.
  • Be grateful for Allah's blessings.
  • Feel compassion for those who experience hunger and hardship.
  • Increase worship and remembrance of Allah.


The Month of Ramadan


Ramadan is one of the holiest months in Islam.
It is the month in which the Qur'an was first revealed to Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ).
During Ramadan, Muslims increase:

  • Prayers.
  • Recitation of the Qur'an.
  • Charity
  • Supplication (Du'a).
  • Good deeds
  • Seeking Allah's forgiveness.
Allah says:
> "The month of Ramadan is that in which the Qur'an was revealed as guidance for mankind..."
(Surah Al-Baqarah 2:185)


Daily Routine of Fasting


Suhoor
The pre-dawn meal eaten before the Fajr prayer.

The Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) encouraged Muslims to eat Suhoor because it contains blessing (barakah).

Iftar
The meal that breaks the fast immediately after sunset (Maghrib). The Sunnah is to break the fast with fresh dates if available, or water.


Who is exempt from fasting?


Islam is a religion of mercy. Some people are exempt from fasting, including:

  • Children who have not reached puberty.
  • Those who are seriously ill.
  • Travelers.
  • Elderly people who cannot fast.
  • Women during menstruation or postnatal bleeding.
Pregnant or breastfeeding women when fasting may harm them or their child (they should follow the guidance applicable to their situation).

Some exemptions require making up missed fasts later, while others may require feeding a needy person if fasting cannot be made up. The exact ruling depends on the reason for the exemption.


Benefits of Sawm


Fasting teaches Muslims to:

  • Control their desires.
  • Strengthen patience.
  • Appreciate Allah's blessings.
  • Care for those in need.
  • Become more disciplined.
  • Draw closer to Allah through worship.


The Night of Decree (Laylat al-Qadr)


One of the greatest nights of Ramadan is Laylat al-Qadr (The Night of Decree).

Allah says:
> "The Night of Decree is better than a thousand months."
(Surah Al-Qadr 97:3)

Muslims seek this blessed night during the last ten nights of Ramadan through extra prayer, Qur'an recitation, and supplication.


Key Lessons


Fasting is an act of worship done for Allah alone.
Ramadan is a month of mercy, forgiveness, and spiritual growth.
Self-control and patience are essential qualities of a believer.
The Qur'an is the ultimate guidance for humanity.
Caring for others and increasing good deeds are central to Ramadan.


5. Hajj (الحج) – The Pilgrimage to Makkah


What is Hajj?

Hajj is the fifth pillar of Islam. It is the pilgrimage to the Kaaba in Makkah, Saudi Arabia, which every Muslim must perform once in their lifetime if they are physically, financially, and safely able to do so.

Hajj takes place every year during the Islamic month of Dhul-Hijjah, the twelfth and final month of the Islamic calendar.

Allah says in the Qur'an:
> "And pilgrimage to the House is a duty owed to Allah by all people who are able to undertake it. But whoever disbelieves, then Allah is free of need from all creation."
(Surah Aal 'Imran 3:97)


Why do Muslims perform Hajj?


Muslims perform Hajj because Allah has commanded it.

Hajj is an act of complete submission to Allah and follows the example of Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham, peace be upon him), his wife Hajar (peace be upon her), and their son Isma'il (Ishmael, peace be upon him).
It reminds Muslims of:

  • Obedience to Allah.
  • Equality of all people before Allah.
  • Unity of the global Muslim community (Ummah).
  • The importance of repentance and seeking Allah's forgiveness.


The Kaabah


The Kaabah is the sacred House of Allah located within Al-Masjid al-Haram in Makkah.

Muslims around the world face the Kaabah when performing their daily prayers.

During Hajj, pilgrims gather around the Kaabah to worship Allah alone.

Muslims do not worship the Kaaba. Rather, they worship Allah, and the Kaabah serves as the Qiblah (the direction of prayer) that unites Muslims in worship.


Main Acts of Hajj


During Hajj, pilgrims perform several acts of worship, including:

Ihram
Entering a sacred state with the intention of performing Hajj and wearing the prescribed clothing.

Tawaf
Circling the Kaabah seven times in worship of Allah.

Sa'i
Walking seven times between the hills of Safa and Marwah, following the example of Hajar (peace be upon her).

Standing at Arafah
Spending time in prayer and supplication on the Plain of Arafah. This is one of the most important parts of Hajj.

Muzdalifah
Collecting pebbles after sunset for the next ritual.

Stoning the Jamarat
Throwing pebbles at the pillars representing the rejection of Satan's temptation.

Sacrifice
Many pilgrims offer an animal sacrifice in remembrance of Prophet Ibrahim's willingness to obey Allah. The meat is distributed to those in need.

Shaving or Trimming the Hair
Men shave or shorten their hair, while women trim a small portion of their hair, marking the completion of key rites.


Who must perform Hajj?


Hajj is obligatory only for Muslims who:

  • Have reached adulthood.
  • Are physically able.
  • Can afford the journey and expenses without neglecting their family's needs.
  • Can travel safely.
Those who are unable due to permanent illness, lack of financial means, or other valid reasons are not sinful for not performing Hajj.


Benefits of Hajj


Hajj teaches Muslims:

  • Sincerity in worship.
  • Patience and perseverance.
  • Humility before Allah.
  • Equality, as pilgrims wear simple clothing regardless of status or wealth.
  • Unity among Muslims from every nation, language, and culture.


Key Lessons


Hajj is the fifth pillar of Islam.
It is required once in a lifetime for those who are able.
It commemorates the devotion of Prophet Ibrahim, Hajar, and Isma'il (peace be upon them all).
The Kaabah is the Qiblah for Muslims, not an object of worship.
Hajj is a journey of worship, repentance, and spiritual renewal.
